Native American Literature

Characteristics

  • Hero Initiation - does not fit in, divine
  • Trickster - human evil, bring out the flaws, bad influence, not necessarily evil (coyote)
  • Symbolic Landmarks - making landmarks objects
  • Oral Tradition - no written language, writing is meant to be performed

    • National Identity

    Advanced sense of culture, they are aware that there are different sides of man, and that man is equal and imperfect; understood that they were one with nature, respect that relationship

    Worldview

    Understanding of and with nature, they know how they rely on nature for their physical, safety, and social needs and know they have to give back and take care of nature in return

    Myth Chacteristics

    • Believed to be sacred
    • Believed to be true
    • Set in the distant past
    • Divine Beings
    • Represent a way of understanding nature
    • Primordial or creation story
    • Flaws of the human character
    • Extra Human, inhuman, or proto human character

European Travel Narrative

National Identity

Superior because of technology; entitled to the land and economic; not going to hear the natives out

Worldview

God, glory, gold; Judeo-Christian with scientific influences; "I will conquer you because I am more powerful"

Characteristics

  • Expedition plagued by intrigue and disunity (leader comes together to unite a lazy crew)
  • Triumph of the explorer
  • Comparison of old life vs. new life
  • Native Americans are barbarians or friendly, no middle ground
  • Reporting advantages of European technology (iron, natives use arrows)
  • Describing new world geography
  • Great Suffering

    • Note of Importance

    Through tweaking reality, Columbus and de Vaca display themselves as heroes; hyperbole for those who opposed them (huge beasts, arrows that could pierce trees) this displays traits of Native American myth, they make themselves extra human and the natives protohuman.

American Puritanism

National Identity

Marks the beginning of the unique, American identity; "doing exactly what you must to survive"

Worldview

Government was a theocracy; Mayflower Compact was a religious, social contract that made people give up liberties in order to be protected; theocracy vs individual freedom was a big theme

American Romanticism

National Identity

Challenges Puritanism; values independence and freedom due to the constraints of the past puritan lifestyle; values nature

Worldview

Marks beginning of a paradigm shift from religious to secularism; explains evil in the human characters through psychological reasons rather than the work of supernatural beings

Characteristics

  • Values feeling and intuition over logic and reason
  • Places faith in inner experience and imagination
  • Shuns the artificiality of civilization and seeks unspoiled nature
  • Prefers youthful innocence to educated sophistication
  • Champions individual freedom and worth of the individual
  • Contemplates nature’s beauty as a path to spiritual and moral development
  • Looks backward to the wisdom of the past and distrusts progress
  • Finds beauty and truth in exotic locals
  • Sees poetry as highest expression of imagination
  • Finds inspiration in myth, legend, and folk culture

American Realism

National Identity

Trying to find your way in this new American frontier ; the journey to self-actualization; trying to find yourself

Worldview

Less religiously driven; put more emphasis on physical world (objectivity is important, social class); normally the setting is usually religiously oriented

Characteristics

  • Renders reality closely and in comprehensive detail
  • Characters are more important than action and plot
  • Characters appear in their real complexity of temperament and motive
  • Class is important (interaction of middle class and immigrants)
  • Events will usually be plausible
  • Diction is natural vernacular
  • Objectivity in presentation becomes increasingly important
  • Interior or physiological realism a variant form

Gothic Literature

Characteristics

  • An atmosphere of gloom, terror, or mystery
  • Elements of the uncanny (unheimlich) that challenge reality, including mysterious events that cause the protagonist to question the evidence of his or her senses and the presence of seemingly supernatural beings
  • An exotic setting isolated in time or space from contemporary life, often a ruined mansion or castle. The building may be associated with past violence and contain contains hidden doors, subterranean secret passages, concealed staircases, and other such features
  • A disturbed or unnatural relation between the orders of things that are usually separate, such as life and death, good and evil, dream life and reality, or rationality and madness
  • Events, often violent or macabre, that cannot be hidden or rationalized despite the efforts of the narrator
  • A hidden or double reality beneath the surface of what at first appears to be a single narrative
  • An interrupted narrative form that relies on multiple methods—inserted documents, letters, dreams, fragments of the story told by several narrators—to tell the tale

Anti-Transcendentalism

Existential Beliefs

Man is born with the stain of the original sin, man is the most destructive force in nature, one can only find God through good works and life experience. There are no Universal truths just individual truths, and there is no oversoul just Heaven and Hell. These keynotes in each philosophy are essential in understanding the foundation of both these beliefs

Moral Beliefs

Transcendentalists oppose this with the belief that God can only be reached though good works and experience. They feel living a good life, doing good deeds, and experiencing life through one's work is the only way to find God; believe man will naturally choose wrong over right if given the choice between the two; God can only be reached though good works and experience; feel living a good life, doing good deeds, and experiencing life through one's work is the only way to find God

Realist/Romanticist Poetry

Whitman

Emphasizes the middle class, common man; individual vs society; inclusiveness and identification. Rejects prejudices, like race and gender

Dickinson

Similar to Whitman, focused on individualism, reflects on loneliness, inspirational moments and death as a positive escape

Slave Literature (Frederick Douglass)

Overview

Transition from individual to group worldview (Douglass reflects on himself yet thinks back to all slaves; draws influence from both realism and romanticism

Characteristics

  • Uses language and symbolism of American middle class culture to express the hypocrisy of the institutions of slavery and racism
  • Knowledge and education is the only way to escape slavery and advance the abolition movement
  • Hypocrisy of Christianity - Slaveholders are taught Christian values and morals then turn around and abuse their slaves
  • Uses pathos to emphasize the horrors of slavery
  • Appeals to the white woman

Regionalism

National Identity

How the author tells the story based on where he lives

Worldview

Not religiously driven; focuses on the characters, dialect, customs, topography

Naturalism

National Identity

How man struggles with heredity and the environment; nature is indifferent to the dilemma of man; every human at his core is an animal; “pessimistic materialistic determinism”

Worldview

Characters are from lower class, uneducated, not given opportunities

Emergent Feminine Voice

National Identity

Challenging the traditional roles of women, female struggle with society, why they don’t fit and don’t belong

Worldview

Main role of a 19th century woman was to be a housewife, raise children, and be complacent to their husbands; EFV writes such as Chopin and Gilman sought to combat this through their works

Modernism

National Identity

Unconscious, stream of consciousness, “moment times”

Worldview

Changing, more individualistic world; the faux American dream includes the accusation of wealth and status, a dream to many and a reality to few; the individual is broken

Harlem Renaissance

National Identity

Great emphasis on art’s reflection of the perceiving mind (ex: jazz, blues, poetry)

Worldview

Striving for civic participation, political equality, and economic and cultural self-determination
